Anthropic [announced Project Glasswing recently](https://www.anthropic.com/glasswing), an attempt to point a mythos at the world's software and find vulnerabilities before attackers do. They have reportedly already turned up thousands of bugs, including a 27-year-old flaw in OpenBSD and a 16-year-old one in FFmpeg. The pitch is that AI has crossed a threshold where it can out-find all but the most skilled human vulnerability researchers, and we'd better get the defenders there first.

I think Glasswing is interesting and overall good. I also think it is going to be deeply insufficient.

## The shape of the problem

Two curves are going vertical at the same time:

1. The amount of code being written is up something like 100x. Most of it is generated, most of it is shipped, much of it is barely read.
2. The rate of zero-day discovery is up drastically, because finding bugs no longer requires a human.

Some people frame the second curve as good news: you can now audit a system and find things humans would miss. That is true. But the population of adversarial actors is, and always will be, larger than the population of non-adversarial ones. So the net effect of "anyone can find vulnerabilities cheaply" is not symmetrical. It favors offense.

Which means every piece of software is now, in practice, targeted by something with state-actor-level capability. At [Laurel](https://laurel.ai) our threat model has assumed this for a while. Most apps do not.

## What I expect to see

A resurgence of compromised devices in the home. Not just your laptop — your washing machine, your doorbell, your TV, the random IoT junk you forgot you plugged in five years ago. Either being used as a launchpad against other systems, or quietly exfiltrating what's going on inside your house.

A lot of people have already given up on caring about that data. The assumption is that your purchases, your location, your messages are already being slurped up by some combination of banks, ad networks, Facebook, your phone carrier, and pick-your-three-letter-agency. Fine. The change is that now there is enough compute to actually *do something* with all of it. The NSA built giant data centers because they didn't yet know how to process what they had. We are now at the point where analyzing all of it is, arguably, trivial.

Imagine an LLM trained well on a single US citizen — their writing, their voice, their relationships, their financial history. It can impersonate them convincingly enough to fool another citizen into committing a crime, leaking information, or sending money. The systems we use to answer "is this a person or a bot" are not built for that.

I expect at least one Laurel employee to be successfully targeted by a state-level actor in the next six months. Not because we're special — because everyone will be.

## Why our existing defenses don't hold

So much of modern security rests on "something you know plus something you have." A password plus a phone. The problem is both halves are getting cheaper to compromise.

The phone half: there are enough core vulnerabilities in Linux that Android and iOS patches can't roll out fast enough to fully cover the window between disclosure and exploit. You don't need to break the phone forever, you just need it for the period before the patch lands.

The knowledge half: once an attacker has the host, they wait for you to paste the password out of your manager, read it from the clipboard, and they're done. Almost every system I can think of assumes that if you have the host, you've won. And getting the host is no longer hard.

The social engineering half is the one that worries me most, because it scales the fastest. I recently had to tell my parents that if anyone calls their phone asking for me, it is *always* an attacker. They got that number because at some point I paid the home phone bill, and at some point AT&T or T-Mobile got hacked, and now there is a graph somewhere linking my name to their landline. Today, an attacker uses that to phish a small piece of info. Tomorrow, a model trained on my voice calls and tells my mom I've been in a car crash in New York and the hospital needs her insurance details. Older systems couldn't impersonate a human well enough for that to land. New ones can.

## The atrophy worry

We've been here before, sort of. Windows in the 90s was a disaster; people got compromised constantly, and over a decade Microsoft and the rest of the industry caught up. Defenses improved, users learned to expect patches, and the steady-state stopped being terrifying. The same thing will happen here. Eventually.

What I am less sure about is whether we keep the muscle to do that catching-up. A lot of our ability to defend ourselves comes from human ingenuity — from people who can sit down and design something that doesn't have the vulnerabilities of the thing it's replacing. If we lose the ability to build genuinely new things, and we only know how to remix the old corpus, we may not patch our way out the way we did last time.

That is the part that keeps me up. Not that the bugs are increasing — they are. Not that the hacks are increasing — they are. Reliability is decreasing and will keep decreasing for a while. That part is just weather.

The part I worry about is whether, by the time we need to invent the next generation of defenses, we still remember how.
